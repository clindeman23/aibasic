🪙 What is a Token?
Tokens are the currency of AI. Every word you type costs tokens. Every word the AI writes back costs tokens. A token is roughly ¾ of a word. For example, "beach" is 1 token and "I love the beach" is about 5. When you hit your daily message limit, you have essentially run out of tokens for the day. Longer prompts with big pasted documents spend tokens fast. Short, focused prompts stretch your daily limit further.
🌊 How Claude Thinks

Claude holds up to 200,000 tokens per conversation. That is roughly a 150-page book worth of content it can read and reason over at once.

  • Reads your entire pasted document before answering. Nothing gets cut off.
  • Extended Thinking mode has Claude work through hard problems step by step before replying
  • Artifacts (games, tools, forms) are generated in one focused burst
  • Best for complex, multi-part tasks. One long prompt beats five short ones.
🏄 How ChatGPT Thinks

ChatGPT can hold about 128,000 tokens in one conversation. That is roughly 96 pages of text it can remember at once.

  • In regular mode, ChatGPT answers quickly. You ask, it answers. Simple.
  • Some ChatGPT models pause and "think" before replying. Picture a student who rereads the question twice before writing. The answer takes longer but is usually better for tricky problems.
  • Making images with DALL-E does not count against your text limit. Images are handled separately.
  • Voice mode works exactly like typing. It just converts your speech into text first, then processes it the same way.
🌴 How Gemini Thinks

Gemini has the biggest memory of the three, over 1,000,000 tokens. That is enough to hold an entire textbook in memory at once.

  • Can read a full Google Doc, spreadsheet, or YouTube transcript without summarizing first
  • Images, video, and audio all count as tokens just like text does
  • Type @gmail or @drive in the chat to pull in your emails and files directly
  • Best when your source material is already in Google. No copy-pasting needed.
🏄 Teacher tip: Paste your notes once at the start and ask for everything in one message. Ask for the rubric, exit tickets, and bell ringers all together. You spend the input tokens once instead of three times, and the AI keeps full context across the whole response.

🐧 Surf School

Every legend started as a beginner. The quality of your AI output depends almost entirely on the quality of your prompt — and these techniques, drawn from real research, will have you shredding in no time.

🌊 Works on all three tools 📚 Research-backed
🤙 The golden rule: Treat prompting as a conversation, not a one-shot request. Write a prompt → review the output → refine with specific feedback → repeat. This loop consistently produces better results than trying to write the perfect prompt on the first try.
🏄 The 5 Essentials of a Good Prompt

1. 🎭 Role — Tell it who to be

Assign a persona to shape expertise and tone. AI responds to roles the way a guest speaker responds to being introduced.

"You are an experienced 8th-grade science teacher who specializes in making complex topics accessible…"

2. 🌊 Context — Set the scene

Include grade level, subject, student background, and any constraints. The more context, the more tailored the response.

"My class is 24 7th graders. 4 have IEPs. We just finished fractions and this is their first introduction to ratios."

3. 🎯 Task — Be specific

Use direct action verbs: create, write, analyze, compare, simplify, rewrite. Vague requests get vague answers.

"Take my lesson objectives and turn them into 5 exit ticket questions — 2 recall, 2 application, 1 where students rate their confidence 1–5."

4. 📐 Format — Say how you want it

Specify exactly how the output should look: bullet list, table, numbered steps, one paragraph, etc.

"Format the output as a table: Day, Objective, Activity, Materials, Assessment."

5. 🚧 Constraints — Set the limits

Tell it what to avoid, word counts, reading level, or any restrictions that matter.

"Keep it under 150 words. No jargon. Avoid multiple choice — open-ended questions only."

🌊 Wipeout vs. Barrel Roll
🤦 Wipeout Prompt
"Make a lesson plan about photosynthesis."
  • ✗ No grade level
  • ✗ No time constraint
  • ✗ No format specified
  • ✗ No student context
  • ✗ Result: generic, unusable
🤙 Barrel Roll Prompt
"Here are my notes and objectives for our photosynthesis unit: [paste notes]. Build these into a 50-minute lesson plan for 8th-grade biology. Include a 10-min hook, 20-min direct instruction with a visual analogy, a 15-min lab, and a 5-min exit ticket. 3 students have IEPs — add accommodation notes."
  • ✓ Grade + subject clear
  • ✓ Time constraints set
  • ✓ Format defined
  • ✓ Student needs included
  • ✓ Result: ready to use
🔥 3 Power Moves

🔗 Chain-of-Thought

Ask the AI to reason step-by-step before answering. This dramatically improves complex responses.

Add: "Think step by step…" or "Walk me through your reasoning first, then give me the final answer."

📋 Few-Shot Examples

Show the AI 1–2 examples of exactly what you want before asking for the real thing. The single biggest quality upgrade most teachers can make.

"Here are two examples of the type of question I want: [examples]. Now create 5 more like these about [new topic]."

🔁 Ask for Alternatives

Don't accept the first response as the only option. Asking for multiple approaches lets you pick the best one for your students.

"Give me 3 different ways to introduce this concept. For each, note the pros, cons, and ideal student type."

🏆 The RISEN Framework
🐧 RISEN is the most practical prompt framework for teachers. It covers every element that makes a prompt work. Use it as a checklist when a prompt isn't delivering.
🎭

R — Role

Who should the AI be? "You are an experienced AP History teacher…"

📋

I — Instruction

What exactly should it do? Clear action verb + task.

🏗️

S — Structure

How should it be formatted? Table, bullets, numbered list?

💡

E — Examples

Provide 1–2 examples of ideal output. Worth more than any other element.

⚙️

N — Nuance

Edge cases, constraints, what to avoid, tone requirements.

🐧 RISEN in Action — Full Example
R: "You are an experienced 10th-grade English teacher."
I: "Here are the quiz questions I wrote on symbolism in The Great Gatsby: [paste questions]. Improve them to include more higher-order thinking."
S: "Format as a table: Original Question | Improved Version | Skill Level."
E: "Example: Original: 'What does the green light represent?' Improved: 'How does Fitzgerald use the green light to critique the American Dream? Support with evidence from the text.'"
N: "Keep 2 recall questions, upgrade the rest to analysis or creative response. No yes/no answers. Appropriate for 10th grade."
🚫 Wipeouts to Avoid

🚫 Too Vague

"Write something about the Civil War" forces the AI to guess everything. Add grade, format, length, and purpose every time.

🚫 Overloading

More than 2–3 distinct tasks in one prompt leads to shallow results on all of them. Break complex projects into separate prompts.

🚫 First Draft = Final

The first response is a starting point. Follow up: "Make it more conversational" or "Shorten this to 3 bullets."

🚫 No Student Context

AI doesn't know your students. Without grade level, subject, and learning needs, you'll get generic content no one can use.

🚫 Trusting Everything

AI confidently produces wrong information. Always verify facts, dates, statistics, and citations before using with students.

🚫 Real Student Data

Never paste real student names, IDs, or personally identifiable information into any AI tool. Use placeholders like "Student A."

🔧 How to Refine a Response
🌊 When the first response isn't quite right, don't paddle back to shore — follow up with specific feedback. These phrases get results:
Too long or complex
Shorten this to [X] sentences / bullet points. Keep only the most important ideas.
Wrong reading level
Rewrite this for a [grade level] student. Use simpler vocabulary and shorter sentences.
Needs more structure
Reorganize this into a table / numbered list / headings and subheadings.
Too generic
Make this more specific to [subject / grade level / topic]. Add concrete examples students in [grade] would recognize.
Wrong tone
Rewrite this in a warmer, more encouraging tone suitable for [audience — e.g., parents / struggling students].
Missing differentiation
Now create two versions of this: one simplified for struggling learners and one extended for advanced students.

Ask for the rubric, exit tickets, AND parent email all in one prompt — one message, three deliverables.

Reserve AI for tasks that would take you 20+ minutes manually — differentiated materials, full rubrics, week-long unit plans.
